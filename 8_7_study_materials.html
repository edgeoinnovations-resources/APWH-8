Explain Drill 1 of 2 — AP Command Verb: EXPLAIN

Explaining Nonviolence as a Political Strategy

KC-6.2.V.A
LO: Unit 8-I · HTS-1: Causation

Explain how nonviolent resistance worked as a strategic tool for political change in the 20th century. Use at least two of the following specific historical leaders: Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., Nelson Mandela.

Nonviolent resistance worked as a strategic political tool because it shifted the moral burden of conflict onto the oppressive system — making the violence of the oppressor visible to domestic and international audiences — while simultaneously imposing real economic and political costs on that system through organized, disciplined mass action.

Gandhi refined this strategy in India through decades of noncooperation: boycotts of British goods, refusal to pay taxes, and mass civil disobedience campaigns like the 1930 Salt March. Each action was designed to make British colonial rule economically costly and politically unsustainable, while the spectacle of British authorities arresting peaceful protesters generated international sympathy and delegitimized empire.

Martin Luther King Jr. applied a similar step-by-step strategic logic to the US civil rights movement: economic pressure first (the Montgomery Bus Boycott imposed financial costs on segregated transit systems), which built mass mobilization (the 1963 March on Washington), which created the political momentum required for legislative change (the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965). The strategy worked because it combined moral clarity with tactical sophistication — each step was designed to build on the one before.

Nelson Mandela led the ANC's nonviolent resistance against South African apartheid for decades; even after the ANC turned to armed resistance in the 1960s and Mandela was imprisoned for 27 years, the moral authority he maintained through his imprisonment ultimately enabled a negotiated transition to multiracial democracy in 1994 — a testament to how sustained nonviolent moral pressure can outlast even direct state repression.

Explain Drill 2 of 2 — AP Command Verb: EXPLAIN

Explaining How Militarized States Intensified Conflict

KC-6.2.V.C
LO: Unit 8-I · HTS-1: Causation

Explain how militaries and militarized states responded to conflict in ways that further intensified it after 1900. Use at least two specific historical examples (Pinochet, Franco, Idi Amin, or the military-industrial complex).

Militaries and militarized states intensified conflict after 1900 because their response to political opposition was not negotiation or reform but overwhelming force — a choice that typically generated further resistance, deepened divisions, and prolonged instability rather than resolving the underlying grievances that had produced dissent.

In Chile, General Augusto Pinochet's 1973 military coup overthrew the democratically elected government of Salvador Allende, immediately killing thousands and "disappearing" tens of thousands more. Rather than ending political conflict, the coup's brutality radicalized opposition, generated international condemnation, and left Chile with deep social divisions that outlasted Pinochet's regime — the repression had intensified rather than resolved the underlying political conflict.

In Spain, Francisco Franco's military coup against the Republic (1936–39) turned a political conflict into a devastating civil war. Franco's victory required three years of warfare and the deaths of hundreds of thousands of Spaniards; his subsequent authoritarian rule suppressed regional identities (Catalan, Basque) so aggressively that it fueled the emergence of armed separatist movements like ETA — meaning that military repression directly contributed to creating the very terrorist violence KC-6.2.V.D describes.

More broadly, the global military-industrial complex — the interconnected relationship between governments, defense industries, and arms traders — increased the scale and lethality of all conflicts by constantly expanding the supply of weapons worldwide, making every local confrontation potentially more destructive.

Apply Activity — Skill 2.B: Sourcing and Situation (HIPP)

Analyze a Primary Source from Topic 8.7

The following excerpts come from an interview with a guerrilla officer in Peru's Shining Path. This source appears in the textbook's "Think as a Historian" feature for Topic 8.7.

"All popular war is violent... We use selective annihilation of mayors and government officials, for example, to destroy the presence of the state and create a vacuum. Then we fill that vacuum."

"We are all prepared to die, and we fear nothing because the armed popular struggle will advance and triumph."

"We are fighting against a neofeudal society where the state has almost no presence in many areas and the masses have long been exploited."

— Shining Path guerrilla officer, Chicago Tribune, July 9, 1989 ("More War Will Bring Peace, Say Peru's Maoists after 15,000 Die")

Complete the HIPP analysis grid below, then click to see model responses.

H — Historical Situation

What broader context shaped when and why this source was produced? What was happening in Peru and globally in 1989?

I — Intended Audience

Who was the speaker trying to reach? How does speaking to a US newspaper (Chicago Tribune) shape what is said?

P — Point of View

What is the speaker's perspective? What does their position as a Shining Path officer lead them to emphasize or omit?

P — Purpose

Why did the speaker give this interview? What are they trying to accomplish by making these statements publicly?

H — Historical Situation: By 1989, the Shining Path had been waging its campaign since 1980 and approximately 15,000 people had died (as the article's title notes). Peru was experiencing severe political and economic instability; the government of Alan García was struggling to contain both the Shining Path insurgency and a collapsing economy. Globally, the Cold War was ending — the Soviet bloc was collapsing — meaning that Maoist revolutionary movements were losing their international ideological context. This historical situation helps explain why Shining Path was seeking international media attention: to legitimize its movement as the Cold War order that had defined revolutionary politics was disintegrating.

I — Intended Audience: The source is an interview published in the Chicago Tribune — an American newspaper with a large, primarily non-Peruvian readership. Speaking to a US audience means the officer frames the struggle in terms that might resonate internationally: "neofeudal society," "masses long exploited" — language that echoes American progressive and left-wing frameworks. The audience shapes the language: the officer is not speaking to Peruvian peasants but to an international audience he wants to convince of the movement's legitimacy.

P — Point of View: As a Shining Path officer, the speaker presents violence as rational, necessary, and strategically sound — not as atrocity but as political science ("selective annihilation," "create a vacuum"). He frames the Peruvian state as illegitimate ("neofeudal," with "almost no presence") and positions Shining Path as the only force that can provide genuine governance. His POV leads him to omit the civilian suffering caused by Shining Path's own attacks, the movement's authoritarian ideology, and the reality that most Peruvians strongly opposed the group. His certainty ("we fear nothing," "will advance and triumph") reflects the ideological commitment of a true believer.

P — Purpose: The officer's purpose is to legitimize Shining Path's violence to an international audience — to reframe what others call terrorism as rational political strategy in service of the oppressed. By speaking publicly to a major US newspaper, he is attempting to: (1) present Shining Path as a political movement with comprehensible goals rather than a terrorist group; (2) delegitimize the Peruvian state as the real source of violence; and (3) generate international sympathy or at least neutrality. This is an act of political communication, not simply reportage.

Limitation of the Source: Precisely because this source reflects the speaker's POV and purpose, it illuminates how Shining Path justified its violence internally — but it cannot give us an accurate picture of the actual human cost of those tactics, the perspectives of the government officials and civilians targeted, or the broader Peruvian population's reaction. It is most useful as a window into the ideology of a violent movement, not as a reliable account of what actually happened.

⚡ AP Skill in Focus — Skill 2.B: Sourcing and Situation (HIPP)

Skill 2.B is the suggested skill for 8.7 for a reason: the entire topic turns on the question of who gets to define "resistance," "terrorism," and "freedom". On the AP exam, HIPP analysis appears in:

  • SAQ Q1 and Q2: You must identify and explain at least one HIPP element of a source
  • DBQ: You must do HIPP analysis on at least 2 documents (aim for 4) to earn the sourcing point

The strongest HIPP explanations: (1) name the specific HIPP element, (2) identify the specific detail from the source that reflects it, and (3) explain how that element shapes the source's meaning or limits its usefulness. Don't just say "the author has a bias" — explain the specific mechanism by which their POV/purpose/situation shapes what they say.

SAQ Practice — 3-Part Short Answer · Source-Based (SAQ Type Q2)

Short Answer Question: Global Resistance After 1900

Use the following primary source and your knowledge of AP World History to answer all parts of the question.

"Our government is not elected by the people. It is not of the people, it is against them... I am not a criminal. I am an Irish republican prisoner of war."
— Bobby Sands, IRA hunger striker, written during his imprisonment in the Maze Prison, Northern Ireland, 1981 (Sands died after 66 days on hunger strike, aged 27)
Part A — Identify

Identify one claim Bobby Sands makes in this source about the nature of the conflict in Northern Ireland.

Sample Response: Sands claims that the British government ruling Northern Ireland lacks democratic legitimacy — it is "not elected by the people" and is "against them" — and that his imprisonment is therefore that of a political prisoner of war, not a criminal.

AP Note: "Identify" requires you to state a specific claim from the source. One clear sentence that names the claim earns the point. You do not need to evaluate or analyze the claim — just identify it with enough specificity.

Part B — Explain

Explain how Sands' point of view as an IRA member shapes the argument he makes in this source.

Sample Response (Skill 2.B — POV): Sands' point of view as a member of the Irish Republican Army — a militant organization seeking to end British rule in Northern Ireland and unite Ireland — leads him to frame the conflict as a war between an illegitimate occupying government and the Irish people, rather than as a matter of domestic criminal justice. Because he understands himself to be a soldier in a legitimate political struggle, the language of "criminal" is unacceptable to him; he insists on the language of "prisoner of war," which would imply that IRA violence is a legitimate act of warfare rather than terrorism. His POV thus produces a source in which violence against the British state is morally justified, the British government is fundamentally illegitimate, and the hunger strike is an act of political defiance rather than self-destruction — all framing choices that reflect his organizational identity and political commitments, and that a British government official would categorically reject.

AP Note: This response earns the HIPP point by (1) naming the specific HIPP element (Point of View), (2) connecting it to a specific aspect of the source (the "prisoner of war" vs. "criminal" framing), and (3) explaining the mechanism by which his POV shapes the source (his identity as an IRA soldier makes the criminal frame illegitimate to him). This is the full three-step HIPP move.

Part C — Explain

Explain one way in which this source's historical situation (Northern Ireland, 1981) limits a historian's ability to use it as evidence about the broader nature of resistance movements in the 20th century.

Sample Response (Skill 2.B — Historical Situation + Limitation): The source's historical situation — a hunger strike by an IRA prisoner in the specific context of British-Irish political conflict in 1981 — limits its usefulness as evidence about resistance movements broadly because Sands' argument is inseparable from the particular nationalist framing of Irish republicanism. His claim that British rule in Northern Ireland is inherently illegitimate reflects a specifically Irish nationalist position that was genuinely contested at the time: many Northern Irish Protestants considered themselves British and supported continued union with Britain. Using Sands' source to generalize about "resistance movements" risks importing his specific POV — that colonial or occupying governments are always illegitimate — into a framework that needs to be evaluated case by case. Additionally, as a source produced under extreme duress (a hunger strike), it represents the most heightened, extreme statement of his beliefs rather than a measured political analysis, which further limits its representativeness for drawing conclusions about resistance movements in general.

AP Note: This Part C earns credit by identifying a specific limitation (the source's particular historical situation and the contested nature of its political claims within Northern Ireland) and explaining the mechanism by which that situation limits the source's usefulness for broader generalizations.

CCOT Analysis — HTS-3 · Reactions to Power Structures · 1900–Present

Global Resistance: What Changed, What Continued

Continuity — What Stayed the Same Change Over Time — What Transformed
Throughout the 20th century, oppressed peoples continued to organize resistance against unjust power structures — the fundamental human impulse to challenge illegitimate authority persisted across all regions and ideologies. The tools and tactics of resistance transformed dramatically: from petitions and strikes in the early 20th century to mass civil disobedience, global media campaigns, and transnational networks in the late 20th century.
Authoritarian states continued to use military force to suppress political opposition throughout the century, from Franco's Spain in the 1930s to Pinochet's Chile in the 1970s to Idi Amin's Uganda. International norms around human rights gradually developed — the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), international tribunals — making state repression increasingly subject to global scrutiny and condemnation.
Non-state groups continued to use violence against civilians as a political tactic throughout the century — terrorism as a strategy predates the 20th century and persisted through it. The scale and global reach of non-state political violence expanded with technology: Al-Qaeda's 2001 attacks demonstrated a capability for transnational, simultaneous mass-casualty attacks that earlier movements lacked.
The fundamental debate about what counts as "legitimate resistance" versus "terrorism" remained unresolved — governments consistently applied the terrorist label to opponents while resistance movements claimed political soldier status. Nonviolent resistance gained growing international recognition as a viable and often more effective alternative to armed struggle — Gandhi's and King's legacies became globally influential models that shaped subsequent resistance movements worldwide.

The most important AP insight for the 8.7 CCOT is that the tools of resistance changed while the fundamental struggle persisted. Gandhi's success with nonviolent mass mobilization in the 1930s–40s did not eliminate violent resistance — it added a powerful new model to the repertoire of resistance movements globally. Similarly, Cold War-era state repression (Pinochet, Idi Amin) represented an intensification of an older pattern of authoritarian violence, accelerated by Cold War superpower support for client regimes. The most sophisticated AP argument for this topic connects 8.7 to Unit 9: in the globalization era, both nonviolent resistance (social media-enabled movements, the Arab Spring) and political violence (global jihadist networks) became transnational in ways that 20th-century versions of these movements were not — a genuine transformation in the nature of resistance itself.

PATH 1
The World Wars as Context for 20th-Century Resistance
▸
"The global resistance movements of the mid-to-late 20th century did not emerge in a vacuum — they were shaped by the experience of two world wars that had..."

The global resistance movements of the mid-to-late 20th century did not emerge in a vacuum — they were shaped by the experience of two world wars that had profoundly undermined the moral authority of established power structures and demonstrated the catastrophic consequences of unchecked state violence. The First World War had revealed that the governments of Europe could send millions of men to their deaths in industrialized slaughter while claiming the mantle of civilization and progress; its aftermath shattered faith in established authority across the political spectrum. The Second World War took this further: the Holocaust demonstrated that the most "civilized" European state could organize systematic mass murder against its own citizens while the international community failed to intervene, and the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki showed that modern states could destroy entire cities in an instant. At the same time, the war's mobilization of colonized peoples — who fought for empires that denied them basic rights — radicalized a generation of future resistance leaders across Asia and Africa. Mohandas Gandhi's moral framework for nonviolent resistance, Martin Luther King Jr.'s application of that framework to American racial injustice, and Nelson Mandela's decadeslong struggle against apartheid all emerged in this post-war context: a world that had witnessed what state power at its most extreme could do, and in which the moral legitimacy of any established order was fundamentally in question.

PATH 2
Colonialism and Its Legacy as the Root of Global Resistance
▸
"The resistance movements of the 20th century cannot be understood apart from the longer history of European colonialism, which had..."

The resistance movements of the 20th century cannot be understood apart from the longer history of European colonialism, which had imposed foreign rule, extracted resources, suppressed local political systems, and denied basic rights to hundreds of millions of people across Asia, Africa, and the Americas for centuries. Resistance to colonial rule was not invented in the 20th century — enslaved people had resisted their enslavement from the beginning, colonized peoples had organized against imperial rule throughout the 19th century, and early nationalist movements had emerged across Asia and Africa by the early 1900s. What changed in the 20th century was the scale, organization, and ideological sophistication of these resistance movements, as colonized peoples gained education in European universities, developed mass political parties, and learned to use the Europeans' own rhetoric of freedom and self-determination against them. Mohandas Gandhi's genius was to synthesize these traditions — drawing on both Hindu concepts of noncooperation and European liberal ideas of rights — into a disciplined mass movement capable of making British rule ungovernable. Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela built on Gandhi's model, applying it to different but structurally similar systems of racial subordination. The violent resistance movements — the Shining Path, Al-Qaeda — also emerged from contexts of perceived colonial or neo-colonial exploitation, arguing that the political and economic structures of the 20th century represented a continuation of colonial domination in new forms.

PATH 3
The Cold War as the Ideological Framework for Resistance
▸
"The global resistance movements of the post-WWII era unfolded within the ideological landscape of the Cold War, which..."

The global resistance movements of the post-WWII era unfolded within the ideological landscape of the Cold War, which divided the world into competing capitalist and communist blocs and transformed every local conflict into a potential front in the global struggle between the United States and the Soviet Union. This ideological polarization profoundly shaped which resistance movements received external support: the United States backed anti-communist authoritarian regimes — including Pinochet in Chile, Franco in Spain, and various military governments across Latin America — providing weapons, training, and political cover for state violence that KC-6.2.V.C describes as militarized states intensifying conflict. The Soviet Union, in turn, supported revolutionary and liberation movements that framed their goals in Marxist terms, from the Shining Path's Maoist guerrilla campaign to anti-colonial movements across Africa and Asia. This superpower involvement distorted the nature of resistance movements: local grievances about poverty, inequality, and political exclusion were frequently reframed in Cold War ideological terms, attracting superpower support but also drawing resistance movements into international proxy conflicts that often made them more violent and more extreme than their original goals required. The result was that the Cold War simultaneously amplified every form of resistance — providing resources, weapons, and ideological frameworks to movements on all sides — while also foreclosing the possibility of purely local resolutions to local conflicts.

Connection 1 (8.7 → 8.8): The nonviolent resistance tradition described in KC-6.2.V.A found its most dramatic 20th-century vindication in the peaceful revolutions of 1989 that ended the Cold War. Poland's Solidarity movement — a trade union that became a mass nonviolent resistance movement against Soviet-backed communist rule — directly applied the step-by-step strategic logic of nonviolent pressure that the video describes for the US civil rights movement: economic pressure (strikes), mass mobilization (10 million members), and ultimately political change (free elections in 1989). The Solidarity model was explicitly inspired by Gandhi and King, demonstrating transnational diffusion of nonviolent tactics. The Soviet state's failure to crush Solidarity — and the failure of Brezhnev Doctrine repression to prevent the cascade of 1989 revolutions — also demonstrated the limits of KC-6.2.V.C: when state repression loses international legitimacy and the will to use unlimited force weakens, nonviolent mass movements can overcome even deeply entrenched authoritarian systems.

Connection 2 (8.7 → Unit 9): The globalization of communication technology transformed both nonviolent resistance and political violence by enabling transnational networks, instant global communication, and real-time international audiences for both protest and atrocity. The Shining Path operated as a local Peruvian insurgency, dependent on physical presence and geographic control; its reach was limited by what it could do within Peru. Al-Qaeda, by contrast, operated as a genuinely transnational organization coordinating simultaneous attacks across multiple countries, using encrypted communications, global financial networks, and the internet to recruit, train, and coordinate members who had never met in person. The globalization that Unit 9 describes — the same technological and economic integration that accelerated cultural exchange and economic growth — also accelerated the global reach of political violence, transforming what had been local or national resistance movements into potentially global ones. The same technologies that enabled the Arab Spring's nonviolent mass mobilization also enabled the Islamic State's propaganda machine.
